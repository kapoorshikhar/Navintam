Revolution is necessary when natural political evolution is supressed to restore justice

Vinayak Damodar Savarkar, popularly known as Veer Savarkar, was born in 1883 in a Brahmin family near Nashik. A fiery nationalist, revolutionary thinker, and social reformer, his life was a testament to unwavering determination for India's freedom and the upliftment of society. His journey, which began in a small village, took him to the forefront of India's struggle for independence, earning him both admiration and controversy.

Savarkar's academic pursuits led him to Gray's Inn Law College in London. During his time abroad, he immersed himself in revolutionary ideologies, drawing inspiration from European nationalist movements, particularly those led by Giuseppe Mazzini . Mazzini's vision of a unified Italy resonated deeply with Savarkar, who saw parallels in India's fragmented political state under British colonial rule. Savarkar was not just a student of history; he sought to rewrite it. He firmly believed that India's independence could only be achieved through revolution and armed rebellion.

The Birth of Abhinav Bharat Society

In 1904, Savarkar co-founded the Abhinav Bharat Society, a secret organization dedicated to Indian independence. This society rapidly grew into a network of revolutionaries across India and abroad. It focused on:

  1. Organizing armed rebellions: Training youth in revolutionary tactics.
  2. Smuggling arms: Facilitating the import of weapons to equip freedom fighters.
  3. Promoting nationalist literature: Circulating banned books and pamphlets to spread awareness.

Under Savarkar's leadership, the society became a powerful symbol of resistance against British colonialism, inspiring young Indians to join the struggle for freedom.

A Pen that Ignited Fires

Savarkar's literary contributions were as impactful as his revolutionary activities. His book, "The First War of Indian Independence" (1909), was a pioneering reinterpretation of the 1857 Sepoy Mutiny. Unlike British historians, who dismissed it as a mere revolt, Savarkar framed it as India’s first organized attempt at independence.

The British banned the book, but its ideas spread like wildfire among Indian nationalists. Copies were smuggled into India, fueling the fire of rebellion in the hearts of many young patriots.

The Madan Lal Dhingra Incident

One of the most dramatic incidents linked to Savarkar was the assassination of Sir Curzon Wyllie by his associate, Madan Lal Dhingra, in 1909. This bold act in London shocked the British establishment and highlighted the determination of Indian revolutionaries.

Though not directly involved in the assassination, Savarkar was arrested the same year for his role in revolutionary activities, including smuggling firearms.

Imprisonment in Cellular Jail: A Tale of Suffering

In 1911, Savarkar was sentenced to two life terms (50 years) and transported to the dreaded Cellular Jail in the Andaman and Nicobar Islands, infamous for its harsh conditions.

Life in Cellular Jail:

  1. Isolation and torture: Prisoners were subjected to solitary confinement, often in cells near the gallows.
  2. Gruesome labor: Savarkar was forced to work in the Kolhu (oil mill) under inhumane conditions.
  3. Psychological trauma: Many prisoners succumbed to insanity or suicide due to the oppressive environment.

Despite the physical and mental toll, Savarkar continued writing during his imprisonment, etching poems and essays on the walls of his cell using thorns and charcoal. His resilience inspired countless freedom fighters.

Post-Release Challenges and Social Reforms

After 13 years in Cellular Jail, Savarkar was released under strict conditions, including a ban on political activities and confinement to Ratnagiri.

In Ratnagiri, Savarkar shifted his focus to social reform:

  • Abolition of untouchability:
    • He championed the integration of lower castes into mainstream Hindu society, long before these issues gained national prominence.
  • Patit Pawan Mandir (1931):
    • Savarkar established this temple, which welcomed all Hindus regardless of caste, challenging orthodox traditions.
  • Promotion of education:
    • He encouraged literacy and vocational training among marginalized communities.

The Controversies Surrounding Savarkar

Savarkar's later years were marred by controversies, particularly allegations of involvement in Mahatma Gandhi's assassination. Though acquitted due to lack of evidence, the accusations tarnished his reputation among certain sections of society. Critics have often accused Savarkar of abandoning revolutionary activities post-imprisonment, but this is a misconception. Surveillance by both the British government and, later, the Nehru-led Indian government severely restricted his activities. Nevertheless, he continued contributing to the nationalist cause through his writings and speeches.



The Legacy of Veer Savarkar Savarkar's legacy is multifaceted:
  1. A fearless revolutionary: His unwavering commitment to India’s freedom remains an inspiration.
  2. A literary giant: His reinterpretation of the 1857 uprising redefined Indian history.
  3. A social reformer: His efforts to abolish untouchability and promote equality were ahead of their time.

While his methods and ideologies remain debated, Savarkar's contributions to India's independence and society cannot be ignored. His life serves as a reminder of the sacrifices and complexities of the freedom struggle—a journey marked by courage, intellect, and resilience.
